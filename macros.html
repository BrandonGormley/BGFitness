Macros

What are Macros or Macronutrients?

Calories are broken down into what we call, Macronutrients. Macronutrients, or as some people call them "macros" are the building blocks of nutrition. They are better known as protein, carbohydrates, and fat. They are the detailed version of calories and are the nutrients your body needs to build a well-nourished and healthy body.

All foods that you eat are made up of a combination of these three. Each macro has its purpose and functionality when it comes to what it does to your body. Below is a breakdown of what each nutrient does.

Total Calories = Protein + Carbohydrates + Fat

Protein: Is the "muscle building" nutrient. It helps to build and repair muscles, organs, skin, blood and different chemicals, like hormones, in your body. Large amounts are commonly found in meat, poultry, fish, dairy, and eggs. Other foods such as nuts, seeds, and whole grains also contain protein but in smaller amounts.

A common rule of thumb is to have 1g of protein per lb of body weight for optimal muscle growth.
Example: If you weigh 200lbs you want to hit 195g - 205g protein daily.

Carbohydrates: This nutrient provides fuel, the energy for your body and brain. Potatoes, breads, rice & pasta are packed with large amounts of carbohydrates. Carbs are also found in all plant foods, fruits, vegetables, legumes, milk, and yogurt.

A common rule of thumb is to let your carbohydrates take up anywhere between 40% - 50% of your daily caloric intake.

Example: If you eat 2,500 calories a day, you want to take in 1,000 - 1,250 calories of carbs.

Fats: This nutrient insulates and protects your bones and organs, acts as backup fuel for energy like carbs, and helps in brain development. Healthy, unsaturated fats are found in oils, avocados, any type of nuts, seeds, and some fatty fish, such as salmon, sardines, and mackeral. Unhealthy saturated fats are found in high-fat beef, pork, butter, full-fat diary, and processed foods, such as cookies and donuts. (Please note, eating unhealthy saturated fats will not ruin your eating habits or break your diet. In life, we have cravings, and it is totally okay to eat these, just not in large amounts or very frequently.)

A common rule of thumb is to let your fats take up approximately 25% of your daily caloric intake.
Example: If you eat 2,500 calories a day, you want your fats to take up about 625 calories of your total calories.

How / Why should you track your Macros?

Many people ask, why should I start tracking my Macros? Knowing the correct amount of macronutrients your body needs to set yourself up for success is step 1 to achieving your health & fitness vision. By setting a goal for exactly how many grams of protein, carbohydrates, and fat you should consume each day, you're setting yourself up for a better weight-loss or weight-gain success. (Depending on your goals.) Unlike restrictive diets or meal plans, counting macros allows you to have more flexibilty, potentially enabling you to have success with a wider range of foods. Meal plans can tire you out by eating the same bland foods day in and day out forcing you to "cheat" on your plan.

Counting your macros is a whole lot easier than you think it is and you will soon realize it takes up no time of your day.

1. Figuring out your daily calorie intake

Just like we discussed before, finding your BMR / AMR will give you a close to accurate estimate as to how many calories you need daily based off of your sex, height, weight, and activity levels.
I find that the following website is the easiest & simplest way to find these numbers:
https://www.calculator.net/calorie-calculator.html
This website will give you your BMR and AMR. Now remember, your AMR will give you the amount of calories your body requires on a daily basis through your activity levels.



2. Decide your macronutrient breakdown

Remember as we had discussed before, the amount of protein should be equal to 1g per lb of body weight. Your carbohydrates and fats can be split upon the rest of your calories. Look at the following breakdown to use as a general rule of thumb:

Example:
Person 1 = 200lbs & AMR(daily calorie intake) = 2,500 calories

Protein:

1g per 1lb of body weight. 200lbs = 200g protein
1g of Protein = 4 calories
200lbs = 200g Protein.
200g * 4 calories per gram = 800 calories.
2,500 calories - 800 calories = 1,700 calories left for Carbs & fats

Carbohydrates:

1g of Carbohydrates = 4 calories
For this example we will say 40% of total calories will take up carbs.
2,500 calories * .4 = 1,000 calories Carbohydrates
1,000 calories / 4 calories per gram = 250g of carbohydrates
1,700 calories - 1,000 calories = 700 calories left for Fats

Fats:

1g of Fats = 9 calories
Now we have 700 calories left for fats, so all you have to do is divide the two numbers.
700 calories left / 9 calories per gram = 77g of fat

Your Macronutrient Breakdown will be:


Protein: 200g
Carbohydrates: 250g
Fats: 77g

*Side Note* When it comes to your fat and carbohydrates, they are both used for fuel, so if you want to have higher carbs and lower fat that is perfectly ok. OR if you want to go higher fats and lower carbs that is also perfectly ok. What ever works best with your body, and makes you feel the best.
